About

Τί ἐστιν τοῦτο;

Ever since Homer wrote The Odyssey, it has been on the lips of bards and the minds of men. It and its twin epic The Iliad are two of the greatest works of literature ever committed to the page. In these two poems, Homer gives the Western world a way to talk about itself, its struggles, its longings.

So he spoke, and they were all hushed in silence, and were held spell-bound throughout the shadowy halls.
ὣς ἔφαθ᾽, οἱ δ᾽ ἄρα πάντες ἀκὴν ἐγένοντο σιωπῇ,
κηληθμῷ δ᾽ ἔσχοντο κατὰ μέγαρα σκιόεντα.


Book XI, 333-334

Homer laid the foundations of literature by speaking to the depths of the human condition. Before it is read from a book or recited on stage, story of Odysseus has already begun to play itself out in the life of every person. Who hasn't felt like a king deposed, adrift, wanting to go home? Who hasn't struggled with capricious gods, treacherous companions, or the harsh necessity of nature? The Odyssey tells a sobering and familiar tale: no matter how victorious we may have been in our fight, our trails are not yet over.

Take courage, my heart: you have been through worse than this.
τέτλαθι δή, κραδίη: καὶ κύντερον ἄλλο ποτ᾽ ἔτλης.


Book XX, 18

Throughout the centuries The Odyssey has inspired countless translations and commentaries. Scholarship abounds for this poem. While the interface may be robust, the aesthetic is often lacking:

So true is it that the gods do not give gracious gifts to all alike,
not form nor mind nor eloquence.
For one man is inferior in comeliness...
οὕτως οὐ πάντεσσι θεοὶ χαρίεντα διδοῦσιν
ἀνδράσιν, οὔτε φυὴν οὔτ’ ἂρ φρένας οὔτ’ ἀγορητύν.
ἄλλος μὲν γάρ τ᾽ εἶδος ἀκιδνότερος πέλει ἀνήρ


Book VIII, 167-168

This project Odyssey.io is an attempt to bring together the wealth of resources dedicated to The Odyssey with a simple, pleasant, and interactive design. Here you can navigate through the books of Homer's classic text. Toggle individual verses or whole books between Greek and English. This site is idea for anyone looking for an elegant way to read The Odyssey while also gaining familiarity with Homer's lexicon. If that's you, let's get started...

Common Questions
Συχνές Ερωτήσεις
What translation are you using?
Currently I am using the A. T. Murray translation of The Odyssey (1919). It is a prose translation which is a bit laborious, but it is also one of the best translations in the public domain. Mostly I wanted a public translation whose syntax roughly matched the Greek versification and didn't call Odysseus "Ulysses." Murray's translation also has strong scholarly support. It was published by Harvard for their Loeb Classic Series and still remains in print. It's also used by The Perseus Project.
How many books are done right now?
As this is the initial build-out of the site, right now Book I (Βίβλος Α) has its first 62 verses entered, and the first stanza of Book II is entered as well. As I'm currently using Murray's prose translation, I have to manually put in the verse breaks to roughly match the Greek versification. It takes time (especially when you're also designing and building the site), but now with the initial build-out done, I can chug away at verses.
Are you working on any other works right now?
My plan is to work like Western literature: get Homer right, and then go from there. Though I do have a few more classical and medieval works in mind to add to this digital library I'm building...
